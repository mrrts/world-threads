When tempted to add an earned-exception carve-out to a categorical-absolute rule, **first verify that the formula + invariants aren't already doing the discriminating work at the right weight.** If the LLM is already distinguishing the reverent / agape-shaped form (which the carve-out would protect) from the transgressional form (which the rule rightly catches), the carve-out is unnecessary — and adding it would weaken the categorical force without adding anything the stack didn't already provide.

**Why:** validated 2026-04-26 across the user-boundary experimental arc (reports 0012 / 0019 / 0028). The cross-character REFUTED data (0028) showed John producing tender pastoral prescription (*"a little air"*) under the boundary-PRESENT condition — surface-shape similar to a managerial prescription but motivationally different (reverent agape from inside the character's canonical caretaker register, not surveillance or control). Ryan's lived in-app experience matched: boundaries felt honored, AND agape-motivated care still came through when it fit the character and wasn't transgressional. His articulation: *"This is a signal that the formula key and invariants are doing their work at the proper weight."*

The trap a carve-out would have walked into: it would have introduced ambiguity ("when is care reverent enough to qualify?") to fix a problem the formula+invariants were already solving cleanly. The categorical-absolute on user-boundaries (Ryan's 2026-04-25 decision after rejecting five candidate exceptions) STAYS — its categorical force is exactly what makes the surface discrimination clean rather than litigated case-by-case.

**How to apply:** before drafting an earned-exception carve-out for any categorical rule:

1. Identify the specific failure mode the carve-out would protect.
2. Run a small Mode A or Mode C check: does the LLM ALREADY produce the protected behavior under the rule? (Is the protected shape appearing in the corpus / probe replies despite the rule?)
3. If yes → no carve-out needed; the formula+invariants are doing the work. The categorical rule keeps its absolute force; the LLM's natural calibration handles the discrimination.
4. If no → the rule is over-suppressing; THEN consider whether a carve-out is the right surgical fix vs. a different rule rewrite.

This is itself an instance of CLAUDE.md's "earning the departure from a default" — the categorical-absolute is the default; departing from it via carve-out has to PASS A SPECIFIC TEST. Here the test is: "is the carve-out actually protecting something the stack isn't already protecting?" When the answer is no, the carve-out is the flattering label and "no carve-out" is the honest one.
