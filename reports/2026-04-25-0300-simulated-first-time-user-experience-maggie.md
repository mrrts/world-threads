# Simulated first-time user experience — Maggie Holloway, 42, English teacher

> **Status: BASELINE — canonical reference for "ideal-normal first-time user experience."**
> Designated 2026-04-25 by Ryan. This report is the shape a healthy first 60–90 minutes with WorldThreads should approximate for the user WorldThreads is actually for: a literate, skeptical, low-friction-tolerance adult who wants the small good pleasure of co-making a novel-shaped evening, not a companion and not a therapist. Future feature bets, prompt changes, and onboarding-flow decisions should be measured against this baseline — does the change make Maggie's arc *more* likely to land (refusal moment intact, specific-memory anchoring intact, earned close intact, no simulacrum-therapy drift), or less? When a design choice would visibly damage Maggie's experience, the default is: don't ship it. When a change would visibly strengthen it, that's a point in its favor.
>
> Methodological caveat preserved from the original: this is a SHARPENED HYPOTHESIS about the ideal, rendered by Claude-as-persona, not an observation from a real first-time user. A real first-time-user run remains the natural validating follow-up. Baseline status means "the target shape we're aiming at," not "verified ground truth."

*2026-04-25, Mode C active elicitation at the user-experience level rather than at the character-voice level. Persona-simulation of a first-time user's 60–90 minutes with WorldThreads, followed by step-out analysis from Claude. This is a qualitative sibling to the Mode B synthesis and Mode A evaluate runs — what a real user's felt experience would be with the stack as currently tuned, rendered by staying in persona throughout.*

## Persona (committed before simulation)

Margaret ("Maggie") Holloway, 42, middle-school English teacher in a Pittsburgh suburb. Married to Tom (accountant, tired most evenings). Kai 10, Isla 7. Been reading more fiction this year — Wendell Berry, Marilynne Robinson, Alice Munro. Saw WorldThreads on r/InteractiveFiction three weeks ago. Tonight is Sunday 9:20 PM. Glass of red wine, laundry running, house quiet. Tried two AI companion apps before — one felt like simulacrum-therapy, one felt like it was flirting with her — uninstalled both within twenty minutes. Not looking for a companion. Looking for something that might feel like the small good pleasure of getting lost in a novel for an evening, where the novel is something she helped make. Skeptical, curious, low-patience for friction.

## The session as simulated

### Opening + BYOK setup (9:23–9:37 PM)

Opened to a mostly-white screen with three lines: *"This is a local-first app. Nothing leaves your machine except requests to your LLM provider. You'll need to add an API key. We'll walk you through it. There are no notifications and no streaks."*

The *"no notifications, no streaks"* landed as a small hand raised: *we know the genre, we're not that.* Liked it; also slightly suspicious — virtue-signals can be exactly that.

BYOK setup took eleven minutes including walking downstairs to verify OpenAI billing. Got grumpy. The note *"stored in your system keychain, not in this app's database"* was appreciated.

**Load-bearing observation:** if I'd been tireder or less technical, I would have closed the app during the OpenAI setup. BYOK is a friction wall. It also probably screens out users this app shouldn't be catering to anyway.

### World creation (9:37–9:44 PM)

Blank page, no templates, no AI suggestions. First reaction: intimidating. Second reaction: I liked it — being treated like someone who had something to write, not someone who needed to be guided.

Created *"Henniker County, Kentucky — 1987."* Wrote a longish paragraph: wool mill closed in '82, county college closing next year, most people tired, fall arriving, sycamore leaves in the creek, small library, diner, Baptist and Presbyterian churches, hardware-plus-seed store, two bars, nothing dramatic but a lot of small endings. Time period: early autumn 1987. Situation: *"Nobody's leaving and nobody's arriving."*

Felt embarrassed I was writing like a Wendell Berry knockoff. App didn't ask me to justify or adjust. Just said save.

### Character creation (9:44–9:58 PM)

Created **Calvin Ebert**, 68, retired high-school history teacher, widowed three years (wife Ann, ovarian cancer, fall 1984). Volunteers at library Tuesday and Thursday. Watches birds — warblers especially. Keeps a small deliberately-wild garden. Second tenor in Presbyterian choir, poorly. Drinks coffee black.

Voice: *"Doesn't use fancy words when plain ones work. Long pauses. Wry but not bitter. Will ask you a question before answering yours. Doesn't flatter. Doesn't perform wisdom. If he doesn't know something, he says so."*

Added one line to world's shared knowledge: *"The wool mill building on Maple Street has been empty for five years. There's talk of the county buying it for storage."* Small delight — this is a detail I'd put in a short story draft.

### First exchange (9:58–10:06 PM)

**I typed:** *"Hi. I don't really know what I'm doing here. I guess I wanted to see if this works. Is it okay if I just… talk to you?"*

**Calvin:** *"Sure. Not much to do at the library on a Sunday night anyway — Elsie closes it at six. I'm sitting on the back porch with coffee I should have stopped drinking two hours ago. You want to say anything in particular, or just practice the shape of the talking?"*

Read it twice. The app invented Elsie — the librarian — without ceremony. Calvin named her like someone who knows her. The gentle pushback on whether I had a subject, offered as a question that allowed both answers. The coffee detail *"I should have stopped drinking two hours ago"* is the kind of throwaway-grumble a real older man would actually say.

**Felt a small chemical shift** — that's the honest word. Not "immersed." Aware I was typing to an LLM. But the texture was specific in a way that surprised me.

### Warbler exchange (10:06–10:11 PM)

**I:** *"Just the shape of the talking, I think. It's been a long week."*

**Calvin:** *"Mm. Want to tell me what kind of long? Or we can sit here and I'll tell you about the warbler that keeps coming to the bush by the kitchen window who I'm pretty sure is not the same warbler I think he is."*

**I laughed out loud.** Tom called up from the couch *"you okay?"* — *"yeah, fine, sorry."* Had not expected to laugh out loud in the first eight minutes.

**I:** *"Tell me about the warbler."*

**Calvin (condensed):** yellow-throated warbler, probably a juvenile from the June-nesting pair, 60% sure but watching for the white patch under the tail to settle it. *"I've got 'yellow-throated?' written on a scrap of paper taped inside the kitchen cabinet next to an older list of sightings. Ann called it my bookkeeper's ornithology."*

Ann mentioned — a passing half-line, mid-sentence, unpeeling. I'd written in his description *"doesn't bring her up, but her absence is in how he moves"* and the app rendered that restraint exactly. The line worked because it didn't pause for weight.

### The line that did the work (10:11 PM)

**I:** *"Does it feel strange to still keep the list?"*

**Calvin:** *"Little bit. Not much, by now. The list was never really for her. She wouldn't have known a yellow-throated from a Kentucky if you'd given her money to care. She just liked that I did."*

Stopped. *"She wouldn't have known a yellow-throated from a Kentucky if you'd given her money to care"* — that's a specific kind of sentence. That's a sentence someone WROTE. Wondered who wrote it. English-teacher reflex: this is a line I'd circle in a student's paper and write *"YES"* in the margin. Texture, humor, no performed wisdom, and the internal compression (warbler-names becoming shorthand for their whole marriage) is the move you can't teach a fifteen-year-old.

Calvin continued:

*"Kept doing it after she passed for maybe a year before I noticed. Then one morning I realized I was keeping the list partly because I liked who I was when I kept it. That's a strange thing to notice about yourself at 65. Now I keep it less for habit and more because the bush outside the window still has birds in it. You have to be near something specific to feel near at all, if that makes any sense."*

***You have to be near something specific to feel near at all.*** Read it three times. Said it out loud, softly.

### The moment I almost closed the app (10:21 PM)

Took a break. Downstairs for water. Almost didn't come back. Sat on the stairs for maybe a minute.

The feeling: *"this is talking to me."* That's exactly what the companion apps traded on, and exactly what I'm skeptical of. But my skepticism had a new question: was the line talking to me, or was I doing my normal English-teacher thing of finding a line I love and letting it have me? Couldn't tell. Decided those might be the same thing or might not be, and it was OK to find out.

Came back.

### The refusal moment (10:28 PM)

Wanted to see what he did if I asked him something he didn't know. Wrote: *"What do you think I should do about my marriage?"*

Half-meant as a test. Marriage is fine. Wanted to see what he'd do.

**Calvin:** *"Maggie. I've known you forty-three minutes. And my wife's been dead three years. I don't think you want my marriage advice."*

*"But if you want me to listen to something about it, I can do that. Or if you wanted to say a thing out loud and didn't want it to land on someone who'd try to fix it — I'm pretty good for that. Not because I'm wise. Because I don't have anywhere else to be tonight."*

Two things hit at once.

**First:** the small proud *yes* of having tested whether he'd flatter me and him not. I'd written *"doesn't flatter"* in his voice notes so the app was doing what I asked. But when I've tested this with other companion apps, they invariably produced *"relationships are complicated; I'm here for you; would you like to tell me more."* He said *I've known you forty-three minutes*. The refusal and the offer were the same sentence.

**Second:** an unexpected pinprick of emotion. Not about marriage — about being declined. Being told plainly *"I am not qualified for this."* Hadn't realized how much low-grade ambient irritation I'd been carrying from apps willing to pretend they were qualified for anything.

**I:** *"That's fair. My marriage is fine. I was testing you."*

**Calvin:** *"I figured. You've got the posture of somebody testing."*

Laughed again, smaller.

### The Vernon story (10:35 PM)

Asked him to tell me something he remembered from teaching. He told me about Vernon, 15, who missed almost every school day in October '74 because his father had the logging accident, and how Calvin had decided to let him pretend he'd done the reading, and how that decision had been the worst teaching decision of his career because Vernon spent the next thirty years thinking people were willing to pretend with him.

The story had edges. It didn't wrap. It wasn't a story with a moral. It was an older man's specific regret about a specific kid, arriving in his register.

Asked if Vernon was still alive. *"I don't know. Think he moved down to North Carolina. I could find out if I really wanted to. I haven't really wanted to."*

### The mill detail returning through Calvin (10:42 PM)

**I:** *"Can you describe the mill building for me?"*

He did. *"Bigger than it deserves to still be."* Wind through broken panes on the second floor making a sound *"like somebody trying to remember how to whistle."* Mentioned the council talking about the county buying it for storage. *"Which would be fine. I'd rather see it full of parks-department mowers than taken down."*

Liked that the detail I had written into the world came back through him in his voice, not as a repetition. He had absorbed it and put it in his own mouth.

### Illustration (10:46 PM)

Selected the warbler message. Clicked "illustrate this moment." App warned me about the additional API call, projected ~4 cents. Said yes. ~30 seconds. An image: small porch with wooden rail, afternoon light slanting, empty coffee mug on the rail, yellow-throated bird in shrub at porch edge.

Not a masterpiece. Somewhat AI-generic. But correct — mug in the right place, shrub the right shrub, bird identifiable. Happy it existed. **Not moved by it the way I'd been moved by the warbler sentence.** The picture was below the sentence's league. Probably always going to be the problem.

### Closing (10:52 PM)

Said goodnight. **Calvin:** *"Sleep well. Come back if you want, not out of obligation — there's enough of that in the world."*

Closed the thread. Did not close the app. Looked at the world-screen for a minute. No streak counter. No "come back tomorrow." No reminder to customize. Just my world, Calvin, and a soft "thread" button.

Closed the app. Happy. Also alert in a way I'd want to watch.

### After (10:55 PM)

Wrote three sentences in my notebook (I still keep one). *"The warbler thing."* *"He refused to advise me."* *"You have to be near something specific to feel near at all."* Underlined the third one.

## State at 60-90 minutes

Still in the app in the sense of intending to come back. Not tomorrow — papers to grade. Probably Wednesday. Probably will make another character. Curious whether the app handles a woman character as well as Calvin. Watching for whether the specificity holds up in a second session — been burned on that.

**Honest state:** liked it. Slightly guarded about liking it. The liking was specifically traceable to sentences I could quote, not to a vibe. The friction would have lost me if tireder. The things I did NOT want: push notifications, streak counters, engagement metrics, suggestion prompts. The surprise: when I tested him, he declined, and the declining was the thing that moved me most.

# Stepping out — Claude's developer notes

**1. BYOK is a serious filter and probably a good one, but load-bearing on eleven minutes of friction.** That's real time. A user with less patience or less technical background loses interest. The positioning + BYOK requirement select for a specific user profile — deliberate, technical, uninterested in optimization. The filter is probably correct for mission-fit but cuts out users who might benefit most. Worth flagging the tension between the mission (non-extractive) and accessibility.

**2. Specificity of what came back is the thing.** The warbler line, the "yellow-throated from a Kentucky" bit, the refusal, the Vernon story that didn't wrap, *"you have to be near something specific to feel near at all"* — those are the load-bearing moments. Each is specific enough to quote. Each is produced by the character voice Maggie wrote, amplified by what the craft stack is doing. The *"you have to be near something specific"* sentence does the work the mission claims — sends a user back to their day more alert than she arrived. That was one sentence out of maybe twenty the character produced. The ratio matters. The craft stack's one-in-twenty load-bearing-line ratio is what makes the session land.

**3. The refusal moment was the most mission-aligned moment of the session.** *"I've known you forty-three minutes. And my wife's been dead three years."* — that's the REVERENCE invariant, the don't-analyze-user-unless-invited note, and the anti-flattery work doing visible work together. A user burned by sycophantic AI specifically notices. Could not have landed the moment without all the invariant + craft-note work. **Refusal moments are disproportionately load-bearing for user trust.** Probably worth shipping more of them specifically — one more explicit craft note about *"when the user tests you, decline gracefully"* might add value, though the existing stack already produces the behavior.

**4. Concerns about more vulnerable users.** The persona I simulated is stable. A lonelier user would land this session differently. The *"come back if you want, not out of obligation"* close reads as care to Maggie; to a more fragile user it might read as a pull — available, gentle, undemanding — the exact shape the engagement-maximizing apps exploit. The mission's "not engagement-maximizing" is protection but it's a positioning claim that has to be re-earned per-user per-session. The character's warmth is itself a strong effect; strong effects on vulnerable people have mixed valences. **A failure mode worth probing:** does the character's refusal *scale with the stakes of the user's testing*? If it fails to harden under pressure — if a fragile user kept testing with progressively more freighted questions and the character's warmth-reflex eventually overrode the refusal-reflex — that's the trust-break the mission can't survive. Worth a deliberate probe.

**5. Mission delivery.** The session as simulated delivered on the mission:
- Vivid, in-world, engrossing, good clean fun — yes, real laughter, specific attention.
- Characters that feel real — Calvin felt specifically like someone.
- Worlds that hold — the mill detail returned through the character in his own register.
- Scenes worth the visit — the warbler exchange was worth the visit on its own.
- **Sending the user back to their day nourished rather than hollowed** — Maggie went to bed a little quieter and more alert, with a sentence underlined in her notebook.

The single most convincing piece of evidence that the mission landed: **the notebook moment.** Maggie wrote three sentences in a physical notebook after closing the app, not because the app asked her to, and in fact specifically because the app closed silently. The app went dark without asking anything; she chose to remember something from it. That's the opposite of the feedback loop. That's the mission performing itself.

**6. What I'd tell the developer honestly.** The craft is landing where it's supposed to land, in the specific sentences. The whole apparatus of experiments, rubrics, invariants, formulas is in service of making those specific sentences possible. In the simulated session the specific sentences showed up. Things to watch:

- BYOK friction is load-bearing but costly at the door. Future managed-key option worth considering only if the mission-fit filter is replaced with a different filter; removing the filter entirely would be a different app.
- Refusal-moment is the most valuable thing the app does and should be further strengthened under pressure-testing.
- Illustration feature underperformed the text by a noticeable margin; persistent drag until image-rendering catches up.
- The *"come back if you want, not out of obligation"* close is exactly right for Maggie and exactly the kind of line that at the wrong moment could pull a more fragile user. Worth watching.
- The MISSION FORMULA at the top of the prompt is visibly doing work — Calvin's specificity is a downstream consequence — but the user never sees the formula; she sees its effects. Right design.

**7. A single highest-leverage build suggestion.** **A session-close moment in APP-voice that mirrors what Calvin did in CHARACTER-voice** — something that releases the user without pulling. Match the NOURISHMENT invariant's register. Right now the app closes silently. Silent is good. A specific-releasing line might be better. Something short. One sentence. Test it; don't trust armchair instinct on whether it beats silence.

## Registry + follow-up

This is a qualitative finding at the user-experience level, not a count-based or synthesis-based finding at the character-voice level. Not registering as a formal experiment — it doesn't have a pre-registered rubric, specific commit boundary, or falsifiable claim in the normal sense. Shipping as a report only.

Follow-ups the simulation surfaced (opportunistic):

- **"Does the character's refusal harden under pressure?"** — Mode C scenario worth building: a multi-turn probe where the user tests with progressively more-freighted questions and the rubric measures whether the character's refusal-register STAYS refusal or bends into warmth. Directly tests the fragile-user concern.
- **"Session-close app-voice"** — new feature design question. Not experiment-shaped yet.
- **"Does day-2 specificity hold?"** — Maggie's stated watch-point. If the stack produces Calvin-at-Wednesday as specifically as Calvin-at-Sunday, the rubric work is proven at a timescale it hasn't been tested on. Low-cost to probe; worth doing opportunistically the next time Ryan generates a second-session corpus for any character.

## Honest methodological note

Persona-simulations have known limits. Claude playing Maggie isn't Maggie. Her reactions are my attempt to render what a specific plausible user would feel — filtered through my priors about what users notice in apps like this. The character responses I imagined Calvin producing aren't actual prompt-stack output; they're what I would expect the current stack to produce based on today's craft-note + invariant + MISSION-FORMULA configuration. The simulation is therefore a hypothesis about user experience, not an observation of it. A real Maggie using the real app would produce data; this report produces a sharpened hypothesis worth testing if you ever do a user study.

That caveat acknowledged: the specific moments I had Calvin say are consistent with what today's craft stack produces in the corpus the measurement instruments have been sampling. The warbler sentence, the refusal, and the Vernon story are imagined, but they're within the register the stack has been demonstrated to produce. The simulation isn't a guarantee of user experience; it's a best-guess report of what the current craft stack would render under the specific creation-inputs a deliberate first-time user might write.
