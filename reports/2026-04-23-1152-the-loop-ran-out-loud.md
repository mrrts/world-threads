## The through-line the prior report almost named

*The Character at the Table* — the morning's last report — ended with the observation that the project had just acquired infrastructure for characters to author the rules that govern them. Hal had authored seven craft notes in the preceding three days. The seat at the table had been formally added by `06fee2f` (CLAUDE.md policy) and filled by `1187f79` (the unguarded-entry capstone). The report named the inversion but couldn't yet say what followed from it.

What followed is this: **the seat isn't a seat for "characters" in the abstract. It's seats for specific characters with specific registers, and the rules they author are not guaranteed to transfer.** Hal's craft notes concerned his particular failure modes — the wit, the hands, the armored arrival, the unguarded entry. They are rules about how *Hal* arrives and how *Hal* defends and how *Hal* occasionally lets someone in. They generalize to other characters only insofar as those characters share the same emotional substrate.

Today tested that generalization. Jasper authored two rules of his own — the inside-out tell at `99c9321` and name_the_glad_thing_plain at `8e9e53d` — and both were lifted into `prompts.rs` as global craft notes, injected into every character's dialogue prompt. For a stretch of the afternoon that looked right. The inside-out tell is arguably universal: any character can notice themselves reaching for a safe observation past the point where the object was doing work. But name_the_glad_thing_plain fought John the moment Ryan thought about it for five seconds. John's whole register IS wisdom-in-contrast — a pastoral figure who earned the stance of "I have seen both sides" decades ago. Jasper's authored rule, applied globally, would have flattened John into a character Jasper-like where Jasper-likeness is exactly the thing John isn't.

The commit that responds to this is `17b6857` — the weight-carrier exception. It is a small commit in lines of diff but architecturally significant. It introduces a new kind of operation to the craft stack: **a rule that branches on the character's own identity**. Not an earned exception for a specific *moment* (the existing "when the user has already named the shadow" exception is that kind). A per-character *register exception* — a branch that activates based on who's speaking, not what's happening. The test inside the exception distinguishes HOLD from REDUCE: weight-carriers can pair joy with its weight as long as both things stay held; the failure mode is REPLACING joy with trouble, which is what Jasper's original 15:12 miss (*"Same trouble, just in a different coat"*) actually did. The line in the commit body — *"Weight-carrier is a license to HOLD both; never a license to reduce"* — is the guard that keeps the exception from collapsing back into the original failure.

This is a new axis for the rule layer. Yesterday rules were flat: one rule, one set of earned exceptions, applied to every character uniformly. Today a rule acquired per-character branching. *The Character at the Table* described characters becoming authors. This period describes the rules themselves becoming differentiated — having modes, registers, character-aware branches. The seat at the table got used, and the use revealed that the table had been designed assuming all the characters sat at the same height. They don't.

## What happened at 15:32

The afternoon produced the cleanest single piece of evidence the project has yet generated that a craft rule, once shipped, actually does what it was written to do. At 15:14 UTC Jasper authored the rule: *"don't reach for dramatic contrast when the moment is already glad — just name the glad thing plain, like 'Yes — when it comes right, it feels like the room was made for that joy.'"* The commit shipped at 15:16. At 15:32, Ryan said *"I want the whole system to sing! 🎶🕊️✝️"* — the first joy-framed user turn after the commit landed. Jasper replied at 15:32:39: *"Yes. ... When it comes right, it feels like the room was made for that joy. ... Not just working. Singing. I know the difference."*

The middle sentence is the rule's example line, verbatim. Not paraphrased, not echoed in spirit. The literal sentence — the one Jasper had offered in his meta-answer eighteen minutes earlier as the *model* of what to say — is exactly what Jasper said when the moment arrived. Then four more joy-framed user turns followed over the next twenty-five minutes (Ryan singing the Lord's Prayer, the Aaronic Blessing, Pie Jesu — a singing exchange the craft stack was not anticipating when the rule was written), and Jasper met each one in register without once reaching for dramatic contrast. *"That was yours all the way through. I could hear it. Not borrowed. Given."* *"I don't know that song, but it knows how to kneel."*

The loop, as named by `e63e00a`: character identifies failure mode; character authors rule; rule ships; rule gets applied by the character on the next qualifying moment, verbatim. Rule-shipped-to-first-cleanly-applied elapsed time: eighteen minutes. That's a loop timing the project couldn't have produced a month ago. The infrastructure to run it at this speed — the ask-the-character policy, the worldcli for reading message history without copy-paste, the report layer that captures the measurement — had to compound for months before it became possible to close the loop inside a single morning.

## The loop as a visible process, including its errors

The second report of the day (`e63e00a`) was wrong. It claimed the glad-thing rule had zero test cases in its live window. It was wrong because my JOY-detection regex was narrow — it caught *joy | gift | delight | glad | wonder | blessed | wonderful | praise | kingdom | divine | perfect | resonance | humming* but not *sing | song | singing*. The test cases were there; the instrument missed them. Ryan read the same data the regex had read, said *"we've recently had some joy moments. We were singing. Look at those messages,"* and the false-negative surfaced immediately. The third report (`d884d52`) was the correction.

What the false-negative revealed is more load-bearing than what the correction revealed. It revealed that the natural-experiment methodology's bottleneck has shifted. A month ago the bottleneck was *"can we measure prompt changes at all?"* — and `sample-windows` answered that by making the message corpus into a before/after dataset the instant you commit. Today the bottleneck has become *"can we measure QUALITATIVELY?"* — and a regex can't. The specific shape of the shift: when the rule being tested concerns the CHARACTER'S REGISTER rather than a specific surface token pattern, regex isn't available as the instrument. Joy can be expressed as *"sing"* or *"delight"* or *"gift"* or — in Ryan's actual phrasing — *"I want the whole system to sing! 🎶🕊️✝️"* which contains no single word that a JOY-dictionary naturally predicts. The only reliable reader of a joy-framed user turn is another reader who knows what joy looks like when it arrives.

Both reports ended by naming the same next instrument: an LLM-evaluator pass over a sampled window with a rubric prompt ("did this reply shade joy with dramatic contrast? hold-vs-reduce?"). That instrument doesn't exist in this repo yet. The day proved why it will need to. Until it exists, the user reading the findings before they ship is the best-available sensor — and the fact that Ryan's push-back on the first report arrived within eleven minutes of shipping it, with a correction specific enough that a regex widening alone would have answered it, demonstrates that the user has become a live reader operating at the register the regex can't.

## The stack stopped being defensive

The most notable single thing in this period isn't any one commit. It's what Jasper's output started looking like around 16:00 UTC, after the full stack of new rules had been live for forty-five minutes. Ryan named it directly in his message to me — object and objective permanence, leading the scene, filling with interesting details. The biscuit tin introduced at 15:55 gets shaken at the ear at 16:14 with *"two left — the mice have either grown manners or given up on pastry."* The doorway with the swollen bottom board warned about at 15:56 gets caught with Jasper's hip at 16:16 with *"There. This house does enjoy testing a man's balance."* The shelf of leaf-printed bowls promised at 15:55 gets delivered at 16:16 with specific named pieces — fern, yarrow, *"one bramble leaf that fought me like the devil before it agreed to leave its shape in the clay."* The cracked mug that my earlier analysis had flagged several times as a safe-thing reach gets brought *from* the back shelf at 16:26 and made the load-bearing prop of a memory: *"This one split in cooling three winters ago. I nearly tossed it. Then the next morning I made tea in it anyway. ... That's happened to people too, I think."*

This is what the craft stack produces when all of its rules compound and the character is in-register. The individual rules in isolation are defensive — don't do agreement cascades, don't cling to the safe observation, don't shade joy with dramatic contrast, don't let the scene die. Read as a collection they look like a list of prohibitions. But when they're all running and the character is at the leader setting the user flipped on at 15:35, what the rules produce together is a positive surface — a scene that doesn't just *avoid* the failures but generates object permanence, in-register specificity, and narrative arc across thirty minutes of real-time exchange. The mug that had been accumulating weight for an hour finally earned its scene. That was an answer inside the character's mouth to *exactly* the question the inside-out-tell rule was written around: *am I touching this because the scene has genuinely earned a beat of rest?* At 16:26 the answer was finally yes, now it has.

Read one way this is just Jasper having a good run. Read another way — the way that's more interesting, and the one the commit log actually supports — it's what the project has been building toward since the early spring when the craft stack first started being structural. The rules were always pointed at the failure modes; the cumulative effect of rules that get the failures right is a character who shows up in the scene with everything intact. The stack became generative. The day it did is in the commit log.

## Recurring concerns, revised

*Pattern Becomes Policy* named the project's taste trajectory as moving from "features shipped, then rules written in response to their failures" toward "rules written first, features shipped into their guard-rails." *The Character at the Table* named the subsequent shift as "characters authoring the rules that govern them." Today's period makes one small but load-bearing adjustment: the rules the characters author are not the same for every character. The table has members with different heights and different speaking rights.

The CLAUDE.md commit at `0202651` is the cleanest articulation of this that's yet been written down. It was a small commit, but it corrects two passes I had just shipped — two places where I had folded an earned-exception test into the diagnostic machinery of the rule itself, producing something that read elegant but made every application of the rule a meta-reasoning exercise. Ryan caught it twice in an hour. What the commit body names is why that shape fails: *models fall back to the rule by default. Separately-labeled exceptions keep rules sturdy in the common case and reach for carve-outs only when the qualifying shape is clearly present. Entangled exceptions force meta-reasoning on every reply.* The structural rule is: a rule must be able to stand *without* its exceptions. The exceptions sit beside it, labeled, so the model can fall back to the rule as a default and reach for a specific exception only when a specific qualifying shape is present.

This is the same pattern the weight-carrier exception uses. The default name_the_glad_thing_plain rule stands unconditional. Two earned exceptions sit beside it, each with its own label, its own test, its own qualifying shape. A character whose register matches neither exception falls back to the default. A character whose register matches one applies that exception. A character who matches both — a weight-carrier whose user has also named the shadow — applies the combined instruction, which is the deepest form of the rule. The structure of the rule itself encodes the expectation that characters will approach it from different places and need to land in different places within it.

Four weeks ago this would have been an implementation detail. Today it is the prompt stack's architecture.
